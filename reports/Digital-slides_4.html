Why alternative punishments — and why now?
The prison problem
Imprisonment dominates criminal justice yet produces severe collateral harms for offenders, families, and communities — and is deployed far beyond what most theorists consider warranted.
Hoskins 2019; Clear & Austin 2009; Krajewski 2016
The mid-level gap
Fines, probation, and community service have failed to displace custody. A shortage of credible, proportionate alternatives is especially acute for mid-level offences where calibration is hardest.
Tonry 1996; Gazal-Ayal & Roberts 2019
Digital life as punishable asset
Internet deprivation and cyberbanishment exploit the constitutive role of connectivity in modern life to generate meaningful punitive burden — as hardship comparable to established sanctions.
Bagaric et al. 2018; Mamak 2023
Key question: Do ordinary people regard digital bans as legitimate, proportionate criminal punishment?
Theoretical background
Punishment theory
Retributivism
Punitive burdens must be deserved and proportionate to the offence. The seriousness of the wrong sets an upper ceiling on severity. (Hart 2008; Ashworth 2010; Duff 1991)
Deterrence & utilitarianism
Burdens justified by future benefits — general and specific deterrence, incapacitation, rehabilitation. Proportionality constraints apply within utilitarian frameworks too. (Tonry 2011; Farrell 2010)
Folk psychology over lab psychology
Criminal law tracks shared social understandings of desert and responsibility — making lay attitudes legally significant regardless of what empirical psychology finds.
Digital punishments
Internet deprivation
Standalone alternative to short custody; 3 days deprivation ≈ 1 day prison. Suited to non-violent offences. The loss of access constitutes genuine and measurable hardship. (Bagaric et al. 2018)
Cyberbanishment
Court-ordered exclusion from specific online spaces; preserves essential access while transferring control from platforms to courts. Meets criteria of hardship, moral acceptability, and enforceability. (Mamak 2023)
Philosophical grounding
Extended mind thesis (Clark & Chalmers 1998), onlife experience (Floridi 2015), and philosophical anthropology (Puzio 2025) position digital access as constitutive of selfhood — not mere convenience.
Hypotheses
H1
Context effect
Online (vs. offline) commission of an offence will increase preference for internet and social media bans relative to other punishments. Directional: ban preferences shift upward, not merely that context matters.

+ 2 contrasts
α = .025 each
H2
Offence type effect
Punishment preferences will differ across the three offence types: financial fraud, stalking, and incitement to suicide. Non-directional omnibus test — the ordering across types is not pre-specified.
LRT χ²(6)
α = .05
H3
Interaction
The effect of offence type on punishment preferences will differ depending on whether the offence was committed online or offline. A significant result triggers exploratory decomposition by worth ratios per offence.

α = .05
H4
Individual differences
Higher punitiveness will predict stronger preference for imprisonment and weaker preference for internet and social media bans. Imprisonment contrast is confirmatory; ban contrasts are theoretically motivated exploratory.

α = .017 each

Analytical strategy
Primary model
Plackett-Luce model
Treats each complete ranking as a sequence of choices. Estimates a worth parameter wi per punishment type reflecting probability of being chosen as most preferred. Implemented via PlackettLuce package in R.
Likelihood ratio tests (H1–H3)
Nested model comparisons: H1 χ²(3) context · H2 χ²(6) offence · H3 χ²(6) interaction. Directional contrasts for H1 via rank-ordered logit (survival::clogit) — one-tailed, Bonferroni-corrected.
Individual differences (H4)
Punitiveness as continuous predictor in rank-ordered logit. Fine as reference level. Imprisonment × punitiveness, internet ban × punitiveness, and social media ban × punitiveness interaction coefficients directly interpretable.
Inference criteria
H1
Omnibus LRT α = .05 two-tailed + two directional contrasts α = .025 each. Both omnibus AND both contrasts must reach threshold.
H2 & H3
LRT α = .05 two-tailed. Non-directional; significant H3 triggers exploratory decomposition (worth ratios per offence, context contrasts within each offence level).
H4
Three one-tailed contrasts, Bonferroni α = .017 each. Imprisonment contrast confirmatory; ban contrasts exploratory (partial support possible).

Key takeaways
1 — Context matters, on a strong traditional baseline
Online offense shifts toward digital bans (H1, all p < .001), but prison + fine still account for ~79% of first choices. Context shifts the distribution without overturning it.
2 — Offense type is the dominant factor
χ²(6) = 259 vs. 72 for context. Intuitive retributivism: financial crime → fine; harassment → SM ban; severe moral harm → prison.
3 — The enforceability bottleneck
Low digital ban preference appears driven by practical skepticism (M = 2.71/7), not principled rejection. Those who believe bans can be enforced are ~1.44× more likely to prefer them.
4 — Punitiveness means imprisonment specifically
More punitive people want more of every sanction in severity ratings, but only prison rises relative to fine in the ranking. Digital bans are not a punitive escalation.

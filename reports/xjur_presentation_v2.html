Conceptual Analysis
What is Conceptual Analysis?
Conceptual analysis takes concepts themselves as its primary subject matter. The analysis of a concept is treated as a crucial step toward understanding the nature of real objects it picks out — not merely the words we use.

Subject

Concepts — not things directly — are the object of inquiry. The word "law" picks out something; the question is what.

Purpose

Conceptual analysis is a crucial step toward understanding the nature of real objects. It precedes and guides empirical inquiry.

Forms

Various forms exist — reductive analysis, connective analysis, Canberra-style analysis — each reflecting different assumptions about what "analysis" consists in and what its outputs should look like.

Taxonomy
Conceptual Analysis in Jurisprudence
Three major approaches structure the field, differing in their assumptions about analysis and its outputs.

Reductive Analysis

Reductionism: reduce legal concepts to more fundamental — typically social or natural — facts, eliminating irreducible normativity.

Connective Analysis

Maps the conceptual landscape: how legal concepts connect to and constrain each other, without reduction to non-legal terms.

Canberra-Style

Combines folk usage data with philosophical elucidation — explicitly incorporates empirical claims about shared usage into the methodology.

Gizbert-Studnicki 2020

Critique
Critique of Conceptual Analysis in Jurisprudence
Michael Giudice (2012) divides critiques of conceptual analysis into three distinct groups, attacking different features of the analytical program:
Argument from plurality of sources — the sheer diversity of law makes any single concept untenable; Brian Tamanaha, William Twining
Argument from irresolvability — persistent disagreement over the concept of law cannot be resolved by conceptual means; Dan Priel, Liam Murphy
Argument from naturalization — empirical inquiry should replace armchair conceptual analysis; Brian Leiter
07 / 18
Critique I
Argument from Plurality of Sources
"The first category of challenges to conceptual analysis is found in the works of those who argue that the sheer diversity of types of law makes pursuit of a single concept of law, which identifies necessary or essential features of law, deeply wrong headed. This group of critics, which is comprised for the most part of legal pluralists, criticizes analytical legal theorists for being unduly narrow and especially state-centric in the range of phenomena chosen from which to elucidate a concept of law. They argue that once the various manifestations and levels of law are acknowledged, it will be clear why there cannot be any single concept or essence of law."
Giudice (2012), pp. 5 — Tamanaha, Twining
08 / 18
Critique II
Argument from Irresolvability
"As Priel contends, there is no way of resolving the disagreement between the two societies and their views about law, and the reason is simple. If it is part of the task of conceptual analysis to explicate the self-understanding of a particular community's view of law, in other words, to explain its concept of law, then if two societies or cultures have different views or concepts of law, which leads them to treat different things as law, at a pre-theoretical or at least pre-reflective level, the best conceptual analysis can do is report that there are multiple, and conflicting concepts of law. This in turn means that any purported necessary or essential features of law are not really necessary or essential features of law at all, but only necessary or essential features of particular, culturally or socially relative self-understandings."
Giudice (2012), pp. 11 — Priel

Critique III
Leiter's Replacement Naturalism

Core Claim

Law is fundamentally a social institution and therefore requires the methods of social science, not armchair conceptual analysis. Modeled on Quine's move in epistemology: just as epistemology should "fall into place as a chapter of psychology," jurisprudence should fall into place as a chapter of sociology or anthropology.

The MCA / ICA Dilemma

Modest conceptual analysis (MCA) — explicating what our shared linguistic conventions tell us about law — produces only glorified lexicography: interesting facts about our concept, not about law itself.

Immodest conceptual analysis (ICA) — inferring law's mind-independent nature from folk intuitions — faces a large and undefended metaphysical investment: what licenses the move from shared usage to real-world nature?

Replacement naturalism is often cited as the theoretical foundation of X-Jur — though this is only partly accurate; it provides motivation for empirical methods without specifying how they should work.

Movement
Experimental Jurisprudence (X-Jur)
X-Jur is a research movement applying the empirical and experimental methods of experimental philosophy to traditional questions in the philosophy of law. Its core motivation: jurisprudence — both general and particular — routinely invokes claims about folk intuitions and ordinary understanding, but traditionally without any empirical verification of those claims.

Central Question

What do ordinary people and legal experts actually think about law? How stable, coherent, and universal are the intuitions jurisprudence presupposes?

Scope

Covers both general jurisprudence (the nature of law as such) and particular jurisprudence (specific legal concepts such as causation, consent, intention, or reasonableness).

Debate
Himma's Objection

The Serious Objection

Folk respondents in X-Jur experiments are unaware of the theoretical implications of their answers and the philosophical assumptions embedded in the questions. For experimental data to bear on conceptual disputes, participants would need to be fully informed of the theoretical stakes — otherwise their responses underdetermine any philosophical conclusion. Connects to an expertise argument: resolving hard cases requires conceptually sophisticated reasoning only legal experts possess.

The Weak Objection Straw Man

Himma interprets X-Jur as attempting to replace traditional analytical methods entirely — and then argues this cannot work. This misrepresents what X-Jur practitioners actually claim. His critique of specific experiments is also selective: he criticizes individual studies in isolation rather than engaging with research programs and series of converging findings.

12 / 18
Reply
Flanagan's Reply
Flanagan's response targets both the serious and weak versions of Himma's objection, arguing that the critique consistently misidentifies what X-Jur is doing.
The claim that intuitions and vignettes are unnecessary or theoretically idle is a mischaracterization — legal theorists themselves routinely invoke folk understandings of law and appeal directly to the intuitions X-Jur investigates.
When jurisprudents use thought experiments and hypotheticals, they are explicitly relying on the very intuitions X-Jur studies systematically and under controlled conditions.
Himma's critique targets what X-Jur is not doing, not what it is actually doing — the target is largely self-constructed.

Framework
Donelson's Positive Framework
Donelson distinguishes three levels of X-Jur activity, each with different epistemic requirements and different degrees of vulnerability to Himma's objection.

Experimental Analysis

Systematic, controlled investigation of folk intuitions on jurisprudential topics — the level Himma primarily engages, where questions about theoretical inference remain live.

Experimental Descriptivism

Investigation of the psychological mechanisms and cognitive processes that generate intuitions (e.g., dual-character concept research by Almeida, Hannikainen, Struchiner). Largely untouched by Himma's objection.

Experimental Restrictionism

Shows that certain methods and assumptions used by legal theorists are undermined by experimental findings (e.g., cross-cultural variation in rule-following intuitions). Does not require folk responses to carry direct philosophical weight.

Model I
Replacement
The most radical model: X-Jur's long-term aim is to replace conceptual jurisprudence, rendering its traditional questions either better formulated or simply obsolete. Analogy: the transition from alchemy to modern chemistry — the newer discipline did not merely supplement but discredited its predecessor.

Weak Reading Himma

X-Jur attempts to resolve conceptual dilemmas by experimental means. Himma argues this cannot succeed because a method cannot dissolve problems generated by a different method.

Strong Reading Leiter

Conceptual jurisprudence is methodologically inappropriate for studying law as a social institution. Just as astronomy introduced frameworks that rendered astrological questions unanswerable rather than answering them, experimental jurisprudence can make the old questions disappear. Critical tension: the strong reading raises the question of whether law's normativity can be touched by any empirical method.

15 / 18
Model II
Integration
Conceptual and experimental jurisprudence are compatible and can form a unified discipline. Analogy: cognitive science — integrating epistemology, psychology, linguistics, and neuroscience without discrediting any of them, by focusing on a shared object of inquiry. The integration thesis rests on a diagnosis: conceptual jurisprudence operates with assumptions about shared concepts and stable intuitions that it has no internal tools to verify.

Empirical Grounding

Transforms hypothetical claims about intuitions into empirically tested ones.

Intuition Mapping

Identifies which intuitions are actually shared across populations, how stable they are, and which vary by culture, expertise, or context.

Bias Detection

Identifies intuitions that are artifacts of cognitive bias or framing effects — allowing conceptual jurisprudence to refine, not abandon, its intuitive resources.

X-Jur under this model is not a rival but a quality-control mechanism for conceptual jurisprudence's empirical presuppositions.

Model III
Independence
The two disciplines are fundamentally different in kind and operate on separate planes — productive cooperation is possible but neither determines nor constrains the other. Analogy: normative ethics and the sociology of morality — both study moral rules, but ask entirely different questions.

The Methodological Divide

Conceptual jurisprudence is a normative discipline — it seeks the correct understanding of concepts and the most adequate account of law's nature.

Experimental jurisprudence is an empirical discipline — it describes patterns in legal cognition, folk concepts, and social behavior.

The Strongest Challenge to Integration

Even if folk intuitions are relevant to conceptual jurisprudence, the inference from "people believe X" to "X is the correct conceptual claim" requires a normative bridge premise that empirical methods alone cannot supply. Independence ≠ isolation: the disciplines can inspire each other and share vocabulary — but cannot adjudicate each other's central claims.

17 / 18
Model IV
Transformation
X-Jur does not replace, integrate with, or operate independently of conceptual jurisprudence — instead, it gradually transforms the questions conceptual jurisprudence asks and the sensibility with which it asks them. The influence is indirect and cumulative. Analogy: literary studies and cultural studies — cultural studies permanently expanded the discipline's interpretive toolkit without replacing literary criticism.

Mechanisms of Transformation

Cognitive humility: experimental findings sensitize jurisprudence to the cognitive constraints of non-expert legal subjects.
Agenda-setting: X-Jur shifts emphasis toward questions with greater social salience or empirical tractability.
Democratization: focus on ordinary citizens pushes jurisprudence from a strongly expert-centered to a more egalitarian orientation.

Distinct from Integration

Transformation is not about X-Jur resolving conceptual problems but about reframing how those problems are perceived and which are worth pursuing. The cumulative result: conceptual jurisprudence becomes more attentive to context-dependence, cognitive variability, and social embeddedness — without abandoning its normative aspirations.

Conclusions
Conclusions
Methodological thesisThe correct relation between analytical jurisprudence and X-Jur is not fixed in advance but is a direct consequence of the method of analysis one adopts.
Four live modelsReplacement, Integration, Independence, Transformation are not competitors for a single truth — they are live options whose appropriateness is keyed to how one understands the aims and methods of jurisprudence.
Canberra-plan caveatCanberra-style analysis explicitly builds empirical claims about folk usage into its methodology — which means the integration model has special prima facie plausibility for anyone working within that framework.
Core questionBefore asking "what is the right relation between X-Jur and conceptual jurisprudence?", one must ask: "What is conceptual jurisprudence for?" The answer to the second question determines which model governs the first.
Methodological transparency requiredThe Himma–Flanagan debate illustrates that the real disagreement is not primarily about X-Jur's empirical findings but about the methodological presuppositions that determine whether those findings count as evidence for jurisprudential conclusions. Researchers in both traditions must be explicit about what kind of claim they are making and what kind of evidence could bear on it.

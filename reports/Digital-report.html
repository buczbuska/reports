Digital Offenses:
Public Punishment Preferences for Online vs. Offline Crime

Full Analysis Report

N = 499  ·  2 × 3 between-subjects design  ·  Plackett-Luce ranking model

1. Introduction & Study Design

This study investigated how people prefer to punish digital offenses — criminal acts committed online — compared with equivalent offline offenses. The central question was: do people think that internet bans and social media bans are more appropriate when an offense was committed online? And does the type of offense change these preferences?

Design

A 2 × 3 between-subjects factorial design. Each participant was randomly assigned to one cell, read one vignette, and completed the full battery.

Measures

For each punishment participants: (1) ranked all four from most to least preferred (drag-and-drop, producing a complete ordering); (2) set an independent severity level; (3) rated how well it serves three penal rationales — just deserts, deterrence, and expressive function (1–6). Individual difference variables (punitiveness, internet reliance, enforceability beliefs, speech restriction concern, political orientation) were measured after the punishment tasks.

  • The prison sentence median (13 months) is far below the mean (29.4), indicating a right-skewed distribution — most people want fairly short sentences, but a minority wants the maximum.
  • Internet and social media ban medians (24 and 36 months) are longer than prison. This may reflect participants treating digital bans as a lighter sanction that requires greater duration to be equivalent in harshness.

  • All distributions have very high SDs relative to their means, indicating substantial individual heterogeneity in severity preferences.

4. Overall Punishment Preferences

The Plackett-Luce Model

Rankings were analysed using the Plackett-Luce (PL) model, which treats each complete ranking as a sequence of choices: at each stage, a person "picks" the most preferred remaining option with probability proportional to that option's worth parameter. Worth values sum to 1 and are directly interpretable as the probability that a punishment would be selected as most preferred in a random pairwise comparison. All hypothesis tests use Likelihood Ratio Tests (LRTs) comparing nested PL models.

5. H1 — Context Effect: Does Online Change Punishment Preferences?

Hypothesis: When an offense is committed online rather than offline, participants will prefer internet ban and social media ban relatively more (compared with fine as the non-digital reference anchor).

Omnibus Test

LRT χ²(3) = 72.14, p < .0001 — The punishment preference distribution differs significantly between online and offline conditions. The omnibus test for H1 is passed.

In the offline condition, prison and fine dominate (combined worth ≈ 0.74). In the online condition, the distribution becomes substantially more equal — all four punishments converge toward 0.25. Social media ban more than doubles in worth, and internet ban increases by over 50%.

6. H2 — Offense Effect: Does Crime Type Change Preferences?

Hypothesis: Different offense types will produce different punishment preference orderings.

Omnibus Test

LRT χ²(6) = 259.28, p < .0001 — Offense type has a massive influence on punishment preferences. This is the largest effect in the entire study, dwarfing the context effect (χ²(3) = 72).

Worth Parameters by Offense Type

PunishmentScamStalkingIncitement to suicide
Prison0.2400.1660.516
Fine0.5950.3270.138
Internet ban0.0970.1970.176
SM ban0.0680.3100.170
Bold = highest worth within each offense. Scam n = 164; stalking n = 166; incitement to suicide n = 169.

Scam: Fine completely dominates (worth = 0.595 — the highest worth value for any punishment in any condition across the entire study). Financial crime → financial punishment. Prison is a distant second (0.240). Digital bans are very low (combined ~0.165).

Stalking: The most balanced profile. Fine leads (0.327), but social media ban surges to 0.310 — nearly equal to fine and well above its overall average of 0.189. Prison is the least preferred punishment (0.166). This is intuitively compelling: stalking involves unwanted contact, and a social media ban directly removes the mechanism. Internet ban is moderate (0.197).

Incitement to suicide: Prison overwhelmingly dominates (0.516 — over half the probability mass). Fine collapses to the bottom (0.138). Digital bans are moderate and similar to each other (IB = 0.176, SM = 0.170). When someone repeatedly encourages a vulnerable person to take their own life, participants demand the most serious sanction available. Financial or digital punishment is seen as inadequate.

H2 verdict: Strongly supported
The "best" punishment changes completely depending on the offense. The preference ordering reverses: for scam, fine is #1 and prison is #2; for incitement to suicide, prison is #1 and fine is #4.

7. H3 — Three-Way Interaction: Does the Context Effect Differ by Offense?

Hypothesis: The shift toward digital punishments in the online condition is not uniform — it is moderated by offense type (context × offense × punishment type three-way interaction).

Tests

Testχ²dfp
Omnibus: 6 cells vs. pooled model356.9715<.0001
Three-way interaction (H3): controlling for main effects25.556.0003
The three-way interaction LRT isolates the interaction by subtracting the log-likelihoods attributable to context and offense main effects separately.

In plain English: the way context (online vs. offline) changes punishment preferences is not the same for all three offenses. The context effect is significantly moderated by offense type, above and beyond what would be expected from the main effects of context and offense alone.

Intuitively: for a scam, moving to the online context cannot shift much because fine already dominates so strongly. For stalking, the already-elevated social media ban preference can be further amplified by an online context. For incitement to suicide, prison's strong dominance acts as a ceiling that buffers the context shift. These differential patterns constitute the three-way interaction.

H3 verdict: Supported
χ²(6) = 25.55, p = .0003. The context effect is not uniform — it depends on what the offense was. Decomposing which specific cells drive this interaction is treated as exploratory follow-up.

8. H4 — Punitiveness: Does Being More Punitive Predict Punishment Preference?

Hypothesis: Higher punitiveness predicts a greater preference for imprisonment over fine (primary preregistered prediction), and exploratorily for internet ban and social media ban over fine.

Punitiveness was measured as the mean of 6 items (1–6 scale; M = 4.40, SD = 0.99). For the continuous analysis, punitiveness was z-scored. Fine is used as the reference item throughout, making the prison × punitiveness coefficient directly interpretable.

9. Enforceability as a Predictor of Digital Punishment Preference

This was a prespecified exploratory analysis. The enforceability item asked whether internet and social media bans can be effectively enforced (1–7; M = 2.71, SD = 1.55 — the sample is strongly skeptical).

People who believe digital bans can actually be enforced are significantly more likely to prefer them. A 1-SD increase in enforceability belief is associated with internet ban being 1.42× more likely to be chosen over fine, and social media ban being 1.46× more likely. The effect is entirely specific to digital punishments — prison preference is unaffected by enforceability beliefs (OR ≈ 1.0, p = .554).

Implication: The low overall preference for digital punishments appears to be at least partially driven by practical skepticism rather than principled opposition. If participants thought the ban would actually work, they endorsed it substantially more. This has direct policy relevance: improving the perceived or actual enforceability of digital bans could shift public support.

  • Prison is the most "costly" punishment. Each month of prison is valued at ~£417, but the IQR spans £200–£1,000 — enormous individual variation.

13. Penal Justification Ratings

For each punishment, participants rated how well it serves three classic justificatory rationales: just deserts (the punishment fits the crime), deterrence (it prevents future offending), and expressive function (it communicates society's condemnation) — all on a 1–6 scale.

Prison scores highest on all three rationales, especially deterrence (4.77) and expressive function (4.78). Participants not only prefer prison for certain offenses — they believe it most effectively communicates moral condemnation and discourages reoffending.

Digital bans score near or below the scale midpoint on all three rationales. Fine is moderate and balanced — it satisfies all three rationales at roughly equal levels, which may explain its broadly acceptable position across very different offense types.

Theoretical tension: Context increases the preference for digital bans (H1), but participants do not strongly endorse them on traditional penal rationales. This suggests the appeal of digital bans in the online context may be driven by an intuitive matching logic ("the punishment fits the medium") rather than fully articulated penal reasoning.

Three Key Patterns

1. Crime-punishment matching is the dominant folk theory (Theme A, n = 69). The single most common objection is that the punishment should target the mechanism of the offense — digital bans are inappropriate when the crime wasn't committed digitally. This reasoning is concentrated almost entirely in offline conditions (56 offline vs. 13 online), and directly explains the context effect found in H1. When offense moves online, this mismatch objection largely disappears, and digital ban preferences rise accordingly.

2. The internet ban is caught in a contradiction (Themes B and C). Some participants find a full internet ban too disruptive to daily life (banking, healthcare, employment — "too much"), while others find it too trivial given the gravity of the crime ("too little"). These objections co-exist, pulling in opposite directions, and together explain why internet ban has lower worth than social media ban even in online conditions — a narrower, more targeted restriction is more acceptable than a total prohibition.

3. Fine and prison face punishment-specific objections that mirror the quantitative data. Fine is rejected for incitement to suicide primarily on moral grounds (Themes F, E, I — you cannot price a human life; £20,000 is trivial; money goes to the state). This directly explains why fine has its lowest worth (0.138) for that offense. Prison is rejected for stalking on proportionality grounds (Theme G — no physical harm, first offense) and counterproductivity grounds (Theme H), consistent with prison having its lowest worth (0.166) for stalking.

The qualitative data validate the quantitative patterns from a completely different angle. The ranking model tells us what people prefer; the explanations tell us why. Every major statistical result in sections 5–6 has a corresponding qualitative rationale identifiable in the free-text data. This convergence substantially strengthens confidence in the interpretation of the quantitative findings.

15. Overall Narrative and Key Takeaways

Context matters — but it operates on a baseline of traditional preference

The headline finding is that when an offense is described as occurring online, participants shift substantially toward preferring digital punishments (H1 fully supported, all effects p < .001). Social media ban more than doubles in worth; internet ban increases by 53%. This is a large and robust effect. But it operates on top of a strong baseline preference for traditional punishments — prison and fine together account for ~79% of first-choice selections across the full sample. Context shifts the distribution but does not overturn it.

Offense type is the dominant factor

Offense type produces by far the largest effect in the study (χ²(6) = 259, vs. 72 for context). Participants engage in a clear punishment-crime matching logic: financial crime → financial punishment; harassment/contact → contact-restricting punishment; severe moral harm → the most severe sanction available. This "intuitive retributivism" shapes preferences more powerfully than whether the offense happened online or offline.

Digital punishments face a dual legitimacy problem

Even when preferred, digital bans score below traditional punishments on every penal rationale (just deserts, deterrence, expressive function). A third of participants flag them as inappropriate. The average enforceability rating is 2.71/7. Yet among those who believe they can be enforced, digital bans are significantly more preferred (OR ≈ 1.44). The problem appears to be practical skepticism, not principled rejection — a meaningful distinction for policy.

Punitiveness is specifically about prison, not punishment in general

More punitive people want more of every punishment (severity analysis), but when forced to choose between punishment types, only their prison-over-fine preference is elevated. Punitiveness, in this context, means wanting liberty-deprivation specifically — digital bans are not perceived as a punitive escalation equivalent to imprisonment, regardless of how long they last.

The enforceability bottleneck

The enforceability finding is perhaps the most policy-actionable result. If public resistance to digital bans is primarily practical (people don't think they work) rather than principled (people think they're wrong in principle), then demonstrating effective enforcement mechanisms could shift public support substantially. This is a testable hypothesis for future work.

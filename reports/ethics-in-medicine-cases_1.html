Topic I

Personhood
& Dignity

Three cases probing the boundaries of moral status, personal identity across time, and the limits of surrogate authority over lives on the threshold of personhood.

Case 1
Personhood & Dignity

Maria, 38 — Persistent Vegetative State

Maria, 38, suffered a severe hypoxic brain injury after cardiac arrest. For two years she has been classified as in a persistent vegetative state. Her husband has been her legal surrogate decision-maker and has consistently refused further aggressive intervention, requesting comfort care only. A new neurologist requests an fMRI as part of a research protocol. The scan reveals that Maria can follow commands and correctly answer yes/no questions. When asked "are you in pain?" she responds yes. When asked "do you want to continue living?" she responds yes. Her husband is not informed of the results immediately — the research team debates whether and how to disclose. Meanwhile, her comfort care plan does not include pain management adequate for a conscious patient.

Q1 — Moral & Legal Status

Does the fMRI finding change Maria's moral and legal status? Did she ever stop being a person with full moral status, or did our epistemic failure constitute a kind of moral failure?

Q2 — Disclosure Obligations

What are the team's obligations regarding disclosure — to her husband, to Maria herself, and to the treating physicians managing her comfort care?

Q3 — Moral Responsibility

Her husband made decisions in good faith based on best available information. Is he morally responsible for inadequate pain management? Who is?

Case 2
Personhood & Dignity

Thomas, 74 — Advance Directive & Dementia

Thomas, 74, was a prominent physician who, at age 65, made a detailed advance directive explicitly refusing all life-sustaining treatment in the event of severe dementia, writing: "the person I will become will not be me in any meaningful sense — do not prolong that person's life on my behalf." Thomas now has advanced Alzheimer's. He does not recognize his family, cannot communicate propositionally, and has no recollection of his advance directive. However, he is observed to be consistently cheerful — he enjoys music, laughs with carers, responds warmly to physical affection, and shows no signs of suffering. He develops aspiration pneumonia. His family, unable to bear losing him, requests full treatment. His daughter, also a physician, invokes the advance directive to refuse treatment.

Q1 — Personal Identity

Is the Thomas who wrote the directive the same person as the Thomas who now enjoys music and responds to affection? Does your answer to that question determine what should be done?

Q2 — Critical vs. Experiential Interests

Dworkin distinguishes "critical interests" (what matters to us reflectively) from "experiential interests" (immediate pleasure and pain). Which should take precedence — and who decides?

Q3 — Binding Future Selves

Does a person have the right to bind their future self — even a future self who appears to have different interests and no access to the prior self's reasoning?

Case 3
Personhood & Dignity

The 23-Week Neonate — Threshold of Personhood

A neonate is born at 23 weeks and 1 day gestation — one day below the threshold at which the hospital's protocol mandates resuscitation attempts. The infant is breathing weakly and has a heartbeat. The parents, both present, are in acute distress and disagree: the mother is begging the team to do everything possible; the father, aware of the statistics, is asking the team to provide comfort care only and allow a natural death. The neonatologist knows that survival at 23 weeks carries roughly a 25% chance, with approximately 50% of survivors experiencing significant neurodevelopmental disability. The hospital protocol says comfort care. The mother says: "she is a person, she is my daughter, you cannot let her die."

Q1 — Moral Personhood

Does the 23-week neonate have full moral personhood? Does your answer depend on her current capacities, her future potential, or something else entirely?

Q2 — Moral Authority

The parents are the legal surrogates but they disagree. The protocol gives a default. Who has moral authority here — and on what grounds?

Q3 — The Clinician's Ethics

Is the neonatologist who follows the protocol and provides comfort care acting ethically? Is the one who overrides the protocol to attempt resuscitation at the mother's request acting ethically? Can both be true?

Topic II

Autonomy

Three cases testing the limits of self-determination: cultural deference, lifestyle choices under public health pressure, and trauma-shaped refusal of compulsory treatment.

Case 4
Autonomy

Amara, 58 — Delegated Decision-Making

Amara, 58, originally from West Africa, is diagnosed with early-stage colon cancer requiring surgery and chemotherapy. When the oncologist discusses treatment options directly with her, she repeatedly redirects: "Please speak to my son — he will decide. In our family, these decisions are made together, and my son is the head of the household." Her son, present at every appointment, is clearly engaged, asks informed questions, and appears to have Amara's welfare at heart. When the oncologist asks Amara privately whether she is comfortable with this arrangement, she says warmly: "Yes — this is how we do things. I trust him completely. This is my choice." Her son ultimately authorizes the recommended treatment.

Q1 — Delegated Autonomy

Amara has explicitly and privately endorsed her son's decision-making authority. Is this an autonomous exercise of her autonomy — choosing to delegate — or a culturally conditioned adaptive preference that undermines the autonomy it appears to express?

Q2 — Relational Autonomy & Cultural Paternalism

The relational autonomy framework asks whether the conditions of Amara's life enable genuine self-governance. Does a Western bioethicist have the standing to judge that her family structure compromises her autonomy — or is that a form of cultural paternalism?

Q3 — Clinical Intervention Threshold

If her son had refused the recommended treatment against what appeared to be Amara's own wishes, at what point and on what grounds would the clinical team be justified in intervening — and how would you establish whose preference was authentic?

Case 5
Autonomy

Marcin, 32 — Lifestyle Autonomy & Soft Paternalism

Marcin, 32, has a BMI of 42 and type 2 diabetes with early signs of nephropathy. His GP has repeatedly advised weight loss, dietary change, and has referred him to a bariatric program. Marcin declines every intervention: "I know the risks. I've read everything. I enjoy my life, I enjoy food, I have good friends, I'm happy. I don't experience my weight as a problem — you do. I'm not in denial, I'm not depressed, I'm not uninformed. This is simply how I want to live." He takes his diabetes medication reliably, attends all check-ups, and is cooperative in every other respect. He asks the GP to stop raising the topic.

Q1 — Frankfurt & Adaptive Preference

Marcin is informed, consistent, and explicitly endorses his lifestyle at the second-order level — he has reflected on it and affirmed it. Does Frankfurt's framework support treating this as fully autonomous? Or is there a residual concern about adaptive preference that justifies continued engagement?

Q2 — Soft vs. Hard Paternalism

The GP finds it professionally and personally difficult to stop raising the issue. Is her continued pressure legitimate soft paternalism — checking that his choice remains genuinely voluntary — or has it crossed into hard paternalism that she is not entitled to exercise?

Q3 — Public Financing & Third-Party Interests

Marcin's future nephropathy will likely create significant costs for the public health system. Does the existence of collective healthcare financing generate third-party interests that limit the scope of his self-regarding lifestyle choices — and if so, where does that logic stop?

Case 6
Autonomy

Dmitri, 44 — TB, Trauma & Compulsory Detention

Dmitri, 44, is diagnosed with drug-resistant tuberculosis following a period of homelessness. He is infectious and requires 18–24 months of directly observed therapy in isolation. He refuses: "I have spent three years without a home and two in prison. I will not be locked up again — not even in a hospital. I will take my medication, I will wear a mask in public, and I will avoid crowded spaces. But I will not be isolated." The public health authority estimates that in his social environment — a dense urban area, frequent use of shelters — his proposed precautions reduce but do not eliminate transmission risk. Compulsory detention is legally available. Dmitri has full capacity. His refusal is informed, consistent, and clearly connected to genuine psychological trauma around confinement.

Q1 — Trauma-Shaped Preference

Dmitri's refusal is grounded in severe psychological trauma — his aversion to confinement is not irrational but is shaped by experiences of profound injustice. Does this history bear on how his autonomy should be weighed — does trauma-shaped preference deserve less, equal, or more deference than ordinary preference?

Q2 — Mill's Harm Principle

Mill's harm principle requires that restrictions be justified by harm to others. Dmitri's proposed precautions reduce but do not eliminate risk. What probability and magnitude of third-party harm is sufficient to override a competent adult's autonomous refusal of detention?

Q3 — Ethics of Non-Detention

Is a public health authority that chooses not to use compulsory detention — accepting residual transmission risk out of respect for Dmitri's autonomy and history — acting ethically? Or does the obligation to protect third parties make non-detention indefensible regardless of the personal cost to Dmitri?

Topic III

Informed
Consent

Three cases examining disclosure standards, systemic accountability, cultural non-disclosure, and the conditions under which silence can — or cannot — constitute valid consent.

Case 7
Informed Consent

Piotr, 67 — Montgomery & Disclosure Failure

Piotr, 67, is admitted the evening before elective hip replacement surgery. A junior doctor arrives at his bedside at 10pm — tired, running behind — and spends four minutes obtaining consent. She mentions infection and blood clot as risks but does not mention nerve damage (1–2% risk) or leg length discrepancy. Piotr, who has waited two years for this surgery, signs without asking questions. He had previously told his GP — whose notes the junior doctor did not read — that he was a keen cyclist and that walking ability was his primary concern. He suffers a nerve injury causing permanent foot drop. His lawyer argues consent was invalid under Montgomery. The hospital argues all risks meeting the professional standard were disclosed.

Q1 — Montgomery Standard

Apply the Montgomery subjective standard: the junior doctor did not know Piotr was a cyclist. Does the disclosure obligation attach to risks that were objectively material to this patient — or only to risks the physician actually knew were material to him? Who bears the burden of finding out?

Q2 — Distributed Responsibility

The junior doctor was operating within a system that allocated four minutes for pre-operative consent before major surgery. Distribute moral responsibility: between the junior doctor, the surgical team, the hospital administration, and the system that produced this process. Is the doctor culpable for a systemic failure?

Q3 — Silence as Consent

Piotr signed the form and asked no questions. From the autonomy framework — does silence following disclosure constitute consent? What would comprehension-based consent, rather than disclosure-based consent, have required here?

Case 8
Informed Consent

Konstantinos, 71 — Cultural Non-Disclosure

Konstantinos, 71, Greek-born, is admitted with a newly diagnosed advanced pancreatic cancer. Before the oncologist can speak to him, his three adult children intercept the team in the corridor: "In our family we do not tell bad news directly. He will lose all hope and die faster. We know him — please tell us first, and we will manage the information." The oncologist, aware that non-disclosure to the patient is standard practice in parts of Southern Europe, agrees to speak to the family first. When she later meets Konstantinos alone, she describes his condition as "a serious illness requiring treatment" without mentioning cancer or prognosis. Konstantinos asks no direct questions. He later discovers his diagnosis from a discharge letter and is devastated — not by the diagnosis, but by the deception.

Q1 — Therapeutic Privilege or Deception?

Was the oncologist practicing therapeutic privilege, respecting a culturally embedded right not to know, or colluding in a deception that violated Konstantinos's autonomy? Can cultural context transform what is otherwise a consent violation into an autonomy-respecting act?

Q2 — The Right Not to Know

Konstantinos asked no direct questions during the consultation. Does his silence constitute a tacit invocation of the right not to know — or does the right not to know require explicit, autonomous invocation by the patient himself?

Q3 — Kant on Lying

Konstantinos says he was devastated by the deception, not the diagnosis. Apply Kant's prohibition on lying: was the oncologist's formulation — "a serious illness requiring treatment" — a lie, a misleading truth, or a defensible omission? Does the distinction matter morally?

Case 9
Informed Consent

Helena, 58 — Contested Surrogate Authority

Helena, 58, suffered a severe stroke leaving her without decision-making capacity. She has no advance directive. Her husband Tadeusz is appointed surrogate decision-maker. The medical team recommends a PEG feeding tube — without it Helena will not survive. Tadeusz refuses: "I know my wife. She spent thirty years as a nurse. She watched patients live for years on tubes and always said she would never want that — she told me this many times. She would rather die." The team has no written evidence of Helena's wishes. Her sister, also present, disputes Tadeusz's account: "Helena never said that to me. She fought for everything in her life. Tadeusz is the one who cannot cope — he wants this to end." The team is uncertain whom to believe. Helena shows some inconsistent responses to stimuli but has not been formally assessed for residual capacity.

Q1 — Evidentiary Threshold for Substituted Judgment

Tadeusz is applying the substituted judgment standard — "what would Helena have wanted." But the evidence for her prior wishes is contested, oral, and filtered through a person with his own emotional stake in the outcome. At what evidentiary threshold should unverified verbal accounts of prior wishes govern life-or-death surrogate decisions — and who should bear the burden of proof?

Q2 — Obligation to Assess Residual Capacity

Helena has not been formally assessed for residual capacity. She shows some inconsistent stimulus responses. Before the surrogate decision proceeds, does the team have an obligation to attempt a capacity assessment — even if the probability of finding meaningful residual capacity is low?

Q3 — Epistemic Uncertainty & Moral Cover

If the team proceeds with the PEG tube against Tadeusz's refusal — acting on best interests rather than substituted judgment — they override the legally appointed surrogate. If they follow Tadeusz's refusal and Helena dies, and it later emerges he fabricated her prior wishes, who bears moral responsibility? Does the team's epistemic uncertainty provide moral cover — or does uncertainty itself generate an obligation to err toward life?

Topic IV

Public Health
Ethics

Three cases examining professional duties in vaccine hesitancy, the ethics of compulsory treatment for structurally vulnerable patients, and the justice of fiscal nudges targeting unhealthy consumption.

Case 10
Public Health

Dr. Monika — Vaccine Hesitancy & Professional Speech

Dr. Monika, a general practitioner in a rural Polish community, has publicly expressed doubts about childhood vaccine safety on social media, citing studies she believes are credible but which are rejected by the mainstream medical consensus. She does not actively discourage vaccination but answers patient questions in ways that leave significant uncertainty — "the science isn't fully settled" and "it's your choice as a parent." Over eighteen months her registered patient population has vaccination rates 30% below the national average. A measles outbreak occurs in her catchment area: 47 children are infected, three are hospitalized, one — a 4-year-old with an undetected immune deficiency — dies. An epidemiological investigation traces the outbreak to the low vaccination cluster in her practice. Dr. Monika has broken no law. Her medical license is under review.

Q1 — Professional Obligation to Communicate Consensus

Dr. Monika never told a single patient not to vaccinate. She communicated uncertainty about a topic where she holds heterodox views. Is there a professional ethical obligation for physicians to communicate public health consensus even when they personally dissent from it — and if so, on what grounds?

Q2 — Parental Autonomy vs. Child Welfare

The parents who declined vaccination exercised formal autonomy — they were not coerced. The child who died had no agency in the decision. How should public health ethics weigh the autonomous choices of parents against the welfare of children who bear the consequences — and third parties harmed by reduced herd immunity?

Q3 — Accountability for Population-Level Harm

Dr. Monika's license review raises the question of professional accountability for population-level harm caused by speech rather than action. Should physicians face professional sanction for public communication that foreseeably undermines population health — even when that communication does not breach any individual patient's rights?

Case 11
Public Health

Andrei, 38 — XDR-TB, Structural Vulnerability & Detention

Andrei, 38, has been diagnosed with extensively drug-resistant tuberculosis (XDR-TB). Treatment requires 18–24 months of directly observed therapy. He has repeatedly failed to attend clinic appointments, taken medication inconsistently, and on two occasions was found working in a crowded food processing facility — a direct transmission risk. Public health authorities have documented six secondary cases plausibly linked to him. He is not malicious: he is a precarious migrant worker who cannot afford to lose his job, does not fully understand the transmission risk despite repeated explanation, and has deep distrust of medical institutions rooted in past experiences of discrimination. Compulsory treatment and detention are legally available. His legal representative argues that detention would be disproportionate and discriminatory.

Q1 — Structural Non-Compliance & Responsibility

Andrei's non-compliance is not wilful defiance but a product of economic precarity, health literacy limitations, and institutional distrust — structural conditions he did not choose. Does the moral analysis of compulsory detention change depending on whether non-compliance is voluntary or structurally determined? And who bears responsibility for the secondary cases?

Q2 — Gostin's Proportionality Framework

Apply Gostin's proportionality framework: is compulsory detention necessary, effective, proportional, and the least restrictive means available? What less coercive alternatives exist — supported housing, income replacement, culturally appropriate community health workers — and why have they apparently not been tried first?

Q3 — Discrimination Claim

His legal representative argues that migrant workers with XDR-TB are disproportionately subject to compulsory detention compared to native-born patients with equivalent non-compliance — making this a discrimination case as much as a public health case. How should public health authorities respond to an argument that their coercive powers are being applied unequally across ethnic or immigration-status lines?

Case 12
Public Health

The Sugar Levy — Fiscal Nudge & Distributive Justice

A large Polish city introduces a municipal levy on sugar-sweetened beverages following a public health campaign documenting rising childhood obesity rates. The levy adds 50 groszy per 250ml to all sugary drinks. Revenue is ringfenced for school nutrition programs and subsidized sports facilities in low-income districts. Evaluation at 18 months shows: overall consumption falls 18%, but the reduction is concentrated in higher-income households; lower-income households reduce consumption by only 6%. Childhood obesity rates in low-income districts are unchanged. The food and beverage industry runs a campaign arguing the tax is regressive — it takes a higher proportion of low-income budgets. A city councillor from a low-income district supports the industry campaign, arguing her constituents are being targeted while wealthy residents barely notice the levy.

Q1 — Regressive Incidence & Distributive Justice

The tax is formally a nudge — it preserves choice while making one option costlier. But for poor households it is a meaningful financial burden. Does the regressive incidence of the tax — taking proportionally more from those it is intended to help — make it an unjust public health intervention regardless of its aggregate effectiveness?

Q2 — Utilitarian Evaluation

The evaluation shows the tax is not reaching the population with the worst outcomes — childhood obesity in low-income districts is unchanged. From a utilitarian perspective: should a public health intervention that fails its target population be extended, reformed, or abandoned? And who bears responsibility for the 18-month gap between introduction and evaluation?

Q3 — Autonomy Against Nudges

The industry argument — that the council has no mandate to "dictate what families drink" — invokes autonomy against a nudge, not a mandate. Is this a principled autonomy objection or a commercial interest dressed in liberal language? How should public health officials respond to autonomy arguments invoked against health interventions but never against commercial food marketing practices that shape the same choices?

Topic V

Organ
Transplantation

Three cases at the intersection of allocation justice, conditional donation, bodily autonomy post-mortem, and the weight of identifiable lives against policy principles.

Case 13
Organ Transplantation

Wojciech, 48 — Re-listing After Relapse

Wojciech, 48, received a cadaveric liver transplant five years ago for alcohol-related cirrhosis after completing a mandatory six-month abstinence program. He remained abstinent for three years post-transplant before relapsing. He is now in acute liver failure from alcohol-related graft damage and requires a second transplant to survive. He is currently sober, cooperative, has entered a residential rehabilitation program, and has strong family support. His transplant team is divided: two members support re-listing, citing his current sobriety, rehabilitation engagement, and the absence of any policy explicitly prohibiting re-listing; two oppose, citing the scarcity of livers, the availability of other patients who have never received a transplant, and concerns about resource stewardship.

Q1 — Desert, Stewardship & Equal Dignity

Should prior receipt of a transplant — and subsequent relapse — affect priority for a second organ? Identify the ethical principle doing the work in your answer: is it desert, resource stewardship, equal dignity, or something else — and is that principle consistently applied across other conditions that involve lifestyle factors and recurrence?

Q2 — Behavioral Conditionality in Allocation

The six-month abstinence rule was designed to predict post-transplant sobriety — but its predictive validity is disputed and it is applied more rigorously to alcohol-related disease than to other behavioral conditions. Is behavioral conditionality in organ allocation a defensible expression of justice — or a form of stigma-driven discrimination that would not survive scrutiny if applied to other lifestyle-related conditions?

Q3 — Retrospective Responsibility

If Wojciech is not re-listed and dies, and it later emerges that his relapse was substantially driven by undertreated depression that the transplant team failed to diagnose — who bears moral responsibility for the outcome, and does retrospective information change the ethics of the original decision?

Case 14
Organ Transplantation

Renata, 34 — Conditional Living Donation

Renata, 34, a healthy non-directed living kidney donor, contacts a transplant center wishing to donate one kidney. During the evaluation process she states that she is willing to donate but has one condition: she does not wish her kidney to go to a patient who has previously refused a transplant for religious reasons and then changed their mind, or to a patient currently on a methadone maintenance program. She is explicit: "I respect their choices, but I want my kidney to go to someone who I feel has made different choices. This is my kidney and I should have some say in who receives it." The transplant coordinator is uncertain how to proceed — directed donation to a named recipient is permitted; directed donation with exclusionary social criteria is not addressed in the center's protocol.

Q1 — Bodily Sovereignty & Donation

Renata has bodily sovereignty over her kidney until donation. Does that sovereignty include the right to attach social conditions to its use — or does the act of donation transfer full allocation authority to the medical system, which must apply its own justice criteria?

Q2 — Complicity in Discrimination

Her exclusion criteria target patients on methadone maintenance — a group already subject to significant stigma and systematic disadvantage in organ allocation. If the transplant center accepts her conditional donation, is it complicit in discrimination? If it refuses her conditional donation entirely, it loses an organ that would otherwise save a life. How should it navigate this?

Q3 — Policy Design

Design a policy that the transplant center could adopt for handling conditional living donations. What conditions, if any, should be permissible — and on what principled basis do you distinguish permissible from impermissible conditions?

Case 15
Organ Transplantation

Tomasz, 29 — Donor Registry vs. Family Refusal

Tomasz, 29, dies in a road traffic accident. He is registered on the national organ donor registry — an explicit, voluntary registration made three years before his death. His parents, who are his next of kin, refuse to permit organ retrieval: "He was our son. We cannot allow his body to be cut up. He was young — he didn't understand what it meant. We are his family and we say no." His partner of four years, who knew about his registration and fully supported it, is present but has no legal standing as next of kin. The transplant coordinator knows that in practice most European centers defer to family refusal even when the deceased was registered — but that in England the opt-out legislation gives donor registration explicit primacy. In this jurisdiction, the law is silent on the conflict. Three patients on the national waiting list are compatible matches. One — a 7-year-old with heart failure — has been waiting 18 months and is deteriorating.

Q1 — Autonomy Post-Mortem

Tomasz made an explicit, voluntary, adult decision to donate his organs. His parents' refusal substitutes their judgment for his on a matter he already decided. On what ethical basis — if any — does next-of-kin authority override a competent adult's prior autonomous decision about their own body after death? Does the answer change if the jurisdiction has opt-out legislation?

Q2 — Relational & Institutional Consequences

The transplant coordinator faces a real-time decision under acute family distress. Even if Tomasz's registered wishes should legally prevail, proceeding over his parents' explicit, grief-stricken refusal has relational and institutional consequences — for the family's wellbeing, for public trust in transplant medicine, and for future donation rates. How should these non-legal considerations factor into an ethically defensible decision?

Q3 — The Identifiable Child

The 7-year-old waiting for a heart is an identifiable third party whose life depends on this decision. Most allocation ethics brackets the identity of potential recipients from donation decisions — but in this case the coordinator knows. Does the presence of an identifiable, deteriorating child change the ethical weight of proceeding against the family's wishes — or does introducing the recipient constitute a form of emotional manipulation that good policy should exclude?

Topic VI

Justice
in
Healthcare

Three cases on what basis should a just society allocate health resources, set coverage limits, and respond to persistent health inequalities?

Case 16
JUSTICE IN HEALTHCARE

Elżbieta, 54 — Pharmaceutical pricing justice

Elżbieta, 54, has been diagnosed with advanced non-small-cell lung cancer with a specific EGFR mutation. Her oncologist tells her that osimertinib — a targeted therapy with strong evidence of extending progression-free survival by approximately 18 months compared to standard chemotherapy — is not funded by her national health system because its cost per QALY exceeds the £30,000 threshold by a factor of three. The drug is available privately at £7,000 per month. Elżbieta has modest savings that would cover approximately four months of treatment before she is bankrupt. Her oncologist is aware that in Germany, the same drug is reimbursed under statutory health insurance. A patient advocacy group is running a crowdfunding campaign for her. The pharmaceutical manufacturer has offered a confidential managed access agreement to the health system at an undisclosed discount — negotiations have stalled for eight months.

Q1 — Formal Equality & Substantive Justice

The QALY threshold that excludes Elżbieta's treatment applies equally to everyone — it is formally non-discriminatory. But it produces different outcomes for wealthy and poor patients, since wealthy patients can bridge the gap privately. Does a formally equal rationing system that produces substantively unequal outcomes satisfy Rawlsian or capabilities-based justice — or does it require explicit correction for background inequality?

Q2 — Pharmaceutical Pricing & Duty-Bearer

The pharmaceutical manufacturer's price is set to maximize shareholder return while capturing maximum healthcare system willingness-to-pay. The patent regime that enables this pricing was designed by wealthy nations partly to incentivize R&D. Does Elżbieta have a justice claim against the pharmaceutical manufacturer — or only against the health system that fails to negotiate a lower price? Who is the relevant duty-bearer when a patient cannot access a life-extending treatment?

Q3 — Clinician as Justice Advocate

Elżbieta's oncologist is considering prescribing the drug through a legally ambiguous mechanism that would allow the patient to receive it at NHS cost while the manufacturer's challenged pricing is under review. Is this physician acting as a justice advocate for her patient, subverting a legitimate allocation system, or both? When healthcare systems produce unjust outcomes, what are the obligations and limits of individual clinician advocacy?

Case 17
JUSTICE IN HEALTHCARE

The Waiting List — Queue justice & priority-setting

A regional hospital's orthopedic surgery waiting list has grown to 18 months for hip replacement — a procedure that dramatically reduces pain and restores mobility but is not life-threatening. The list contains 340 patients. The hospital has received additional funding to clear the backlog and must decide how to prioritize. Current policy allocates by time on list (first come, first served). A review committee is considering three alternatives: (A) prioritizing by severity of functional impairment; (B) prioritizing working-age patients on grounds of greater economic and social productivity value; (C) prioritizing by social vulnerability — patients who are isolated, lack caring support, or whose condition most severely affects daily life. A patient who has waited 16 months and is relatively mobile objects to any reordering. A 78-year-old widow who has waited only 4 months but is severely impaired and isolated would move to near the top under criteria A and C.

Q1 — Procedural vs. Substantive Fairness

First-come-first-served is procedurally fair but substantively indifferent to severity of need. Prioritizing by functional impairment is more responsive to need but violates procedural expectations of those already waiting. Which principle of queue justice is more defensible — and does the answer change depending on whether the queue is for an elective or an emergency procedure?

Q2 — Social Utility & Equal Dignity

Option B — prioritizing working-age patients for their productivity value — is the most explicit application of social utility criteria to elective care allocation. Construct the strongest version of the utilitarian argument for this approach. Then identify the principle it violates most severely — and explain why that violation cannot be justified by the productivity gain.

Q3 — Legitimate Expectations & Retroactive Rule Change

The 16-month waiter has a legitimate expectation interest based on his position in the queue — an interest created by the system's own prior commitment to first-come-first-served. If the hospital retrospectively changes the criteria and he is displaced, does he have a justice claim for compensation or remedy — and does it matter that the change is motivated by greater equity rather than administrative convenience?

Case 18
JUSTICE IN HEALTHCARE

The Vaccine — Global health justice & vaccine nationalism

A novel respiratory pathogen with a 3% case fatality rate has emerged. A vaccine has been developed by a consortium of European publicly funded research institutions and a private pharmaceutical company that received €2.3 billion in public funding, including advance purchase commitments from EU member states. The vaccine is highly effective. The EU has pre-purchased 800 million doses — enough to vaccinate its entire adult population twice over. Low-income countries, primarily in Africa and South Asia, have placed orders through COVAX that will not be fulfilled for 14 months due to manufacturing constraints. During this period, the pathogen is expected to kill approximately 400,000 people in low-income countries who would have been protected by the vaccine. The EU member states have declined requests to share doses or waive IP rights, citing their own population protection obligations. The WHO has declared a pandemic of international concern and called for equitable vaccine distribution.

Q1 — Public Funding & Distribution Obligations

The vaccine was developed using substantial public funding — taxpayers in EU member states funded the research whose fruits now belong primarily to those same member states. Does the public funding origin of the vaccine generate additional justice obligations regarding its distribution — beyond whatever general obligations wealthy nations may have to address global health emergencies?

Q2 — Pogge's Negative Duty & Institutional Harm

EU pharmaceutical IP regimes, advance purchase agreements that crowd out developing country access, and WTO rules preventing compulsory licensing were all designed and maintained by wealthy nations. Is the projected 400,000 deaths a harm that EU member states are actively causing through institutional design — generating a justice obligation beyond charity — or is national governments' primary obligation to their own citizens legitimately prior?

Q3 — The Global Original Position

If parties in the Rawlsian original position did not know whether they would be born in Germany or Nigeria, what vaccine distribution rules would they choose — and how does the answer compare to the distribution system that obtained during COVID-19? What institutional changes would be required to implement the Rawlsian choice — and who has the power to resist those changes and why?

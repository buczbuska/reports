Why alternative punishments — and why now?
The prison problem
Imprisonment dominates criminal justice yet produces severe collateral harms for offenders, families, and communities — and is deployed far beyond what most theorists consider warranted.
Hoskins 2019; Clear & Austin 2009; Krajewski 2016
The mid-level gap
Fines, probation, and community service have failed to displace custody. A shortage of credible, proportionate alternatives is especially acute for mid-level offences where calibration is hardest.
Tonry 1996; Gazal-Ayal & Roberts 2019
Digital life as punishable asset
Internet deprivation and cyberbanishment exploit the constitutive role of connectivity in modern life to generate meaningful punitive burden — as hardship comparable to established sanctions.
Bagaric et al. 2018; Mamak 2023
Key question: Do ordinary people regard digital bans as legitimate, proportionate criminal punishment?
Theoretical background
Punishment theory
Retributivism
Punitive burdens must be deserved and proportionate to the offence. The seriousness of the wrong sets an upper ceiling on severity. (Hart 2008; Ashworth 2010)
Deterrence
Burdens justified by expected future benefits — general deterrence, specific deterrence, incapacitation. Proportionality constraints apply within utilitarian frameworks too. (Tonry 2011)
Expressivism
Punishment expresses society's condemnation of the wrongdoer and communicates the seriousness of the norm violation. The communicative content of a sanction matters independently of its deterrent or retributive function. (Duff 2001; Feinberg 1970)
Folk psychology over lab psychology
Criminal law tracks shared social understandings of desert and responsibility — making lay attitudes legally significant. (Tobia 2022)
Digital punishments
Internet deprivation
Standalone alternative to short custody; 3 days deprivation ≈ 1 day prison. Suited to non-violent offences. Loss of access constitutes genuine, measurable hardship. (Bagaric et al. 2018)
Cyberbanishment
Court-ordered exclusion from specific online spaces; preserves essential access while transferring control from platforms to courts. Meets criteria of hardship, moral acceptability, and enforceability. (Mamak 2023)
Philosophical grounding
Extended mind thesis (Clark & Chalmers 1998), onlife experience (Floridi 2015), and philosophical anthropology (Puzio 2025) position digital access as constitutive of selfhood — not mere convenience.
Hypotheses
H1
Context effect
Online (vs. offline) commission of an offence will increase preference for internet and social media bans relative to other punishments. Directional: ban preferences shift upward, not merely that context matters.

H2
Offence type effect
Punishment preferences will differ across the three offence types: financial fraud, stalking, and incitement to suicide. Non-directional omnibus test — the ordering across types is not pre-specified.

α = .05
H3
Interaction
The effect of offence type on punishment preferences will differ depending on whether the offence was committed online or offline. A significant result triggers exploratory decomposition by worth ratios per offence.

α = .05
H4
Individual differences
Higher punitiveness will predict stronger preference for imprisonment and weaker preference for internet and social media bans. Imprisonment contrast is confirmatory; ban contrasts are theoretically motivated exploratory.

Analytical strategy
Primary model
Plackett-Luce model
Treats each complete ranking as a sequence of choices. Estimates a worth parameter wi per punishment type reflecting probability of being chosen as most preferred. Implemented via PlackettLuce package in R.
Likelihood ratio tests (H1–H3)
Nested model comparisons: H1 χ²(3) context · H2 χ²(6) offence · H3 χ²(6) interaction. Directional contrasts for H1 via rank-ordered logit (survival::clogit) — one-tailed, Bonferroni-corrected.
Individual differences (H4)
Punitiveness as continuous predictor in rank-ordered logit. Fine as reference level. Imprisonment × punitiveness, internet ban × punitiveness, and social media ban × punitiveness interaction coefficients directly interpretable.
Inference criteria
H1
Omnibus LRT α = .05 two-tailed + two directional contrasts α = .025 each. Both omnibus AND both contrasts must reach threshold.
H2 & H3
LRT α = .05 two-tailed. Non-directional; significant H3 triggers exploratory decomposition (worth ratios per offence, context contrasts within each offence level).
H4
Three one-tailed contrasts, Bonferroni α = .017 each. Imprisonment contrast confirmatory; ban contrasts exploratory (partial support possible).

Key patterns driving the interaction
Scam — minimal context shift
Fine already captures 0.595 of probability mass offline. The context effect has almost nowhere to go — digital bans rise only slightly (0.068 → 0.068 SM; 0.097 → 0.097 IB).
Stalking — largest context shift (key signal)
SM ban nearly doubles (0.115 offline → 0.263 online). When stalking is framed as digital, the punishment that removes the mechanism of harassment becomes clearly preferred. Fine and prison both fall.
Incitement to suicide — ceiling effect
Prison dominates at 0.516 offline and 0.459 online. The moral gravity of the offense anchors prison preferences so strongly that the online framing cannot dislodge it meaningfully.

Essential to daily life — banking, healthcare, employment. Explains why internet ban has lower worth than SM ban even in online conditions: a narrow ban is more acceptable than total prohibition.

Insufficient for the gravity of the crime, especially incitement to suicide. Themes B and C pull in opposite directions — both reduce digital ban worth.

Strongest for stalking and scam — no physical harm, first offense. Explains prison's lowest worth (0.166) for stalking.

Cannot price a human life. 19/22 entries from incitement to suicide condition — explains fine's lowest worth (0.138) for that offense.
Methodological note
Free-text responses (N = 345) were coded using keyword-assisted LLM analysis into 11 thematic categories. Coding is exploratory and not subject to formal inter-rater reliability checks. Treat as qualitative triangulation of the quantitative findings, not as independent confirmation.
Convergence with quantitative results
The qualitative data validate the quantitative patterns
Every major statistical result in H1–H2 has a corresponding qualitative rationale in the free-text data. The mismatch theme explains H1. Fine's collapse for incitement (H2) is explained by moral-inappropriateness objections. Prison's low worth for stalking is explained by disproportionality objections.
The internet ban contradiction
Themes B and C co-exist for the internet ban — simultaneously "too much" (disrupts essential life) and "too little" (insufficient for grave crimes). These opposing objections explain why the internet ban has lower worth than the more targeted social media ban even in online conditions.
Intuitive retributivism confirmed
The ranking model tells us what people prefer; the free-text explains why. The punishment-crime matching logic found in the mismatch theme directly mirrors the statistical interaction in H2 and H3. Both converge on the same underlying folk theory of justice.

Key takeaways
Context matters, on a strong traditional baseline
Online offense shifts preferences toward digital bans (H1, both contrasts p < .001, OR = 2.06 and 3.07). But prison + fine still account for ~79% of first choices overall. Context shifts the distribution without overturning it.
Offense type is the dominant factor
χ²(6) = 259 dwarfs context χ²(3) = 72. Participants engage in intuitive retributivism: financial crime → fine; harassment → SM ban; severe moral harm → prison. Qualitative data confirm this is an active matching logic.
The enforceability bottleneck
Low digital ban preference is driven by practical skepticism (M = 2.71/7), not principled rejection. Participants who believe bans can be enforced are ~1.44× more likely to prefer them. This is a testable, addressable policy lever — not an immovable attitude.
Punitiveness means imprisonment specifically
More punitive people want more of every sanction in severity ratings, but only prison rises relative to fine in the ranking model (OR = 1.64, p < .001). Digital bans are not perceived as a punitive escalation — they are a qualitatively different category of sanction.
